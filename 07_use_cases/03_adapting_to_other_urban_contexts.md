# Adapting the Model to Other Urban Contexts

## Purpose

This guide explains how the instructional model can be implemented in urban environments that differ from the original context presented in this repository. The model does not depend on the presence of a Chinatown or any particular cultural district. Instead, it relies on learners engaging with authentic environments that provide meaningful opportunities for observation, interaction, interpretation, and language use.

The objective is therefore to identify locally relevant spaces where language learning can become situated within authentic social, cultural, or institutional contexts.

---

# Core Pedagogical Principles That Remain Constant

Regardless of the location, the following principles should remain unchanged:

- Learning is situated in authentic environments.
- Tasks require purposeful observation and interaction.
- Learners produce multimodal artefacts linked to specific places.
- Digital technologies support spatial mediation rather than replacing real-world experience.
- Assessment is integrated throughout the learning process.
- Learner-produced artefacts may become reusable Open Educational Resources (OERs).

These principles define the instructional architecture of the model and remain valid across all urban settings.

---

# Selecting Appropriate Learning Environments

Suitable learning environments should provide opportunities for authentic communication, cultural interpretation, and multimodal documentation.

Possible locations include:

- historic city centres;
- museums and galleries;
- public markets;
- transport systems;
- parks and public spaces;
- university campuses;
- libraries;
- cultural centres;
- commercial districts;
- multilingual neighbourhoods;
- community organisations;
- local festivals and public events.

The educational value lies in the authenticity of the communicative context rather than in the specific location.

---

# Adapting the Learning Tasks

Tasks should respond to the characteristics of the selected environment while maintaining the same pedagogical objectives.

| Environment | Possible learning tasks |
|-------------|------------------------|
| Historic district | Produce heritage trails, historical narratives, or guided tours. |
| Museum | Describe exhibits, create audio guides, or compare cultural perspectives. |
| Public market | Document products, conduct interviews, analyse multilingual signage, or record cultural practices. |
| University campus | Produce orientation guides, service maps, or student information resources. |
| Public transport | Create multimodal mobility guides or investigate multilingual communication. |
| Public park | Develop ecological trails, cultural narratives, or location-based storytelling. |

---

# Digital Technologies

Digital tools should continue to support spatial learning rather than becoming the focus of the activity.

Suitable technologies include:

- Google Maps;
- ArcGIS StoryMaps;
- ThingLink;
- Genially;
- Wakelet;
- Padlet;
- QR codes;
- augmented reality applications;
- institutional learning platforms.

Technology should facilitate observation, documentation, collaboration, and dissemination.

---

# Assessment Considerations

Assessment should continue to prioritise:

- communicative effectiveness;
- contextual appropriateness;
- multimodal integration;
- collaboration;
- reflective learning;
- quality of learner-produced artefacts.

Assessment criteria should evaluate how effectively learners engage with their chosen environment rather than comparing different locations.

---

# Implementation Checklist

Before implementation, verify that:

- ☐ the selected environment offers authentic communicative opportunities;
- ☐ learning tasks require meaningful interaction with the environment;
- ☐ multimodal production remains central;
- ☐ digital technologies support spatial mediation;
- ☐ assessment focuses on learning processes as well as final products;
- ☐ learner-produced artefacts can be shared or reused whenever appropriate.

---

# Key Takeaway

The instructional model is transferable because it is grounded in situated learning rather than in a specific geographical location. Any urban environment capable of supporting authentic communication, cultural observation, and multimodal production can become an effective learning space when the underlying pedagogical principles are preserved.
