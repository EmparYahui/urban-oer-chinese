# Adapting the Model to Other Languages

## Purpose

This guide explains how the instructional model presented in this repository can be transferred to the teaching of other second and foreign languages while preserving its pedagogical foundations. Although the original implementation was designed for beginner learners of Chinese, the model itself is language-independent. Its effectiveness lies in the integration of authentic urban contexts, task-based learning, multimodal production, spatial mediation, collaborative learning, and open educational practices rather than in language-specific content.

The aim is not to replicate the original intervention, but to preserve its instructional architecture while adapting linguistic content, communicative objectives, learning resources, and assessment criteria to different educational settings. The model can therefore be implemented in any language and across a wide range of learner profiles, institutional contexts, and proficiency levels.

---

# Core Pedagogical Principles

Successful transfer depends on preserving the instructional architecture rather than reproducing the original classroom activities. The following principles constitute the pedagogical core of the model and should remain unchanged regardless of the target language.

## 1. Situated Learning

Learning activities should be embedded in authentic environments where language is used to observe, interpret, and interact with the surrounding world. The urban environment is not simply a backdrop but an active learning resource.

## 2. Task-Based Learning

Language development should emerge from meaningful communicative tasks with clear purposes and authentic outcomes rather than from isolated grammar or vocabulary practice.

## 3. Multimodal Learning

Learners should communicate through multiple semiotic resources—including written language, photographs, audio, video, maps, and augmented reality—to construct meaning across modes.

## 4. Collaborative Knowledge Construction

Learning should incorporate peer interaction, collaborative problem solving, feedback, and co-construction of meaning throughout the instructional sequence.

## 5. Iterative Assessment

Assessment should function as a continuous process supporting revision, reflection, and progressive improvement rather than solely measuring final performance.

## 6. Open Educational Practice

Whenever appropriate, learner-produced artefacts should become reusable Open Educational Resources (OERs), extending their educational value beyond the original classroom.

# What Can Be Adapted?

While the pedagogical principles should remain stable, most implementation decisions can be modified according to local needs.

| Component | Examples of adaptation |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Target language | Chinese, French, German, English, Arabic, Japanese, etc. |
| Proficiency level | CEFR A1–C2 or equivalent curricular frameworks |
| Learning context | Schools, universities, language centres, teacher education |
| Urban setting | Historic districts, markets, museums, neighbourhoods, transport systems, cultural institutions |
| Digital tools | Google Maps, ArcGIS StoryMaps, Padlet, Genially, ThingLink, Wakelet, Miro, institutional platforms |
| Communication platforms | WhatsApp, Microsoft Teams, Moodle, Canvas, Google Classroom, institutional messaging systems |
| AR technologies | Adobe Aero, GeoGebra AR, QR codes, ThingLink, mobile AR applications |
| Assessment instruments | Rubrics, portfolios, reflective journals, peer assessment, digital portfolios |
| Final artefacts | Interactive maps, digital guides, podcasts, subtitled videos, exhibitions, websites, OERs |

# Example Adaptations

## French

Students document multilingual public signage, cafés, museums, and cultural institutions while producing descriptions, interviews, and digital city guides in French.

## German

Learners investigate urban mobility, sustainability, and public services, creating multimodal reports and interactive maps in German.

## Arabic

Students analyse linguistic landscapes in Arabic-speaking commercial areas, documenting cultural practices through photographs, audio recordings, and geolocated narratives.

## English

Learners produce multimedia walking guides, interviews, podcasts, and digital storytelling projects centred on local communities and urban heritage.

## Japanese

Students explore Japanese cultural spaces, restaurants, bookshops, and festivals while developing multimodal artefacts linked to authentic communicative situations.

# Recommended Implementation Workflow

1. Select an authentic urban or community context.
2. Define communicative objectives aligned with the curriculum.
3. Design task-based learning activities.
4. Integrate multimodal production throughout the project.
5. Encourage collaboration and peer feedback.
6. Use formative assessment during the production process.
7. Publish selected learner artefacts as Open Educational Resources whenever appropriate.
8. Reflect on the implementation and adapt the model for future iterations.

---

# Implementation Checklist

Before starting, verify that the instructional design:

- ☐ is organised around authentic communicative tasks;
- ☐ uses meaningful real-world contexts;
- ☐ integrates multimodal production;
- ☐ promotes collaboration and peer interaction;
- ☐ incorporates formative assessment and revision;
- ☐ encourages learner autonomy;
- ☐ produces authentic communicative outcomes;
- ☐ enables publication or reuse of learner-generated artefacts whenever feasible.

---

# Key Takeaway

The instructional model is transferable because its educational value resides in its pedagogical architecture rather than in the specific language being taught. Teachers should therefore preserve the underlying design principles while adapting linguistic content, learning resources, technologies, and assessment procedures to their own educational contexts.
