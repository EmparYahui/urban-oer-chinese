# Adapting the Model to Other Languages

## Purpose

This guide explains how the instructional model presented in this repository can be adapted to the teaching of different second and foreign languages without altering its pedagogical foundations. Although the original implementation was developed for beginner learners of Chinese, the model itself is language-independent. Its effectiveness derives from the integration of authentic urban contexts, task-based learning, multimodal production, spatial mediation through digital technologies, and open educational practices rather than from language-specific content.

The objective is therefore not to replicate the original intervention, but to preserve its underlying design principles while adapting linguistic content, communicative objectives, learning resources, and assessment criteria to new educational settings. Whether implemented in French, German, Arabic, Japanese, English, or other languages, the instructional sequence can be reconfigured to accommodate different curricula, learner profiles, institutional contexts, and levels of linguistic proficiency.

The following sections distinguish between the essential pedagogical components that should remain unchanged and the contextual elements that can be modified to meet local educational needs.

## Core Pedagogical Principles That Should Be Preserved

The transferability of the instructional model depends on preserving its pedagogical architecture rather than reproducing its original activities. While linguistic content, learning materials, and digital tools may vary across educational contexts, the following design principles constitute the core of the model and should remain unchanged.

### 1. Authentic Context

Learning activities should be grounded in meaningful real-world environments that provide opportunities for authentic language use. Although the original implementation was situated in Valencia's Chinatown, comparable contexts may include local neighbourhoods, museums, markets, transport systems, cultural institutions, or virtual environments when fieldwork is not feasible.

### 2. Task-Based Learning

Language should be learned through purposeful communicative tasks rather than isolated grammar or vocabulary exercises. Each activity should require learners to observe, interpret, negotiate meaning, and produce language in response to authentic situations.

### 3. Multimodal Production

Learners should create artefacts that integrate multiple modes of communication, such as written language, images, audio, video, maps, or augmented reality. Multimodal production encourages learners to connect linguistic knowledge with contextual, visual, and cultural information.

### 4. Reflection and Revision

Learning should incorporate iterative cycles of drafting, peer feedback, teacher feedback, and revision. Reflection is not limited to final evaluation but forms an integral part of the learning process.

### 5. Open Educational Practice

Whenever possible, learner-produced artefacts should extend beyond classroom assessment by becoming reusable educational resources. Publishing selected outputs as Open Educational Resources promotes authentic audiences, encourages learner ownership, and supports future teaching and learning.
