# Online and Hybrid Implementation

## Purpose

This guide explains how the instructional model can be implemented in online and hybrid learning environments while preserving its pedagogical foundations. Although the original intervention relied on face-to-face urban exploration, the model can be successfully adapted when direct fieldwork is limited or impossible.

The objective is not to replicate the physical experience exactly, but to preserve authentic communication, situated learning, multimodal production, and collaborative knowledge construction through appropriate digital mediation.

---

# Core Pedagogical Principles That Remain Constant

Regardless of the delivery mode, the following principles should remain unchanged:

- Learning is organised around authentic communicative tasks.
- Learners actively construct knowledge through observation and inquiry.
- Multimodal production remains central throughout the learning process.
- Collaboration supports meaning-making and problem solving.
- Assessment is continuous and formative.
- Learner-produced artefacts may become reusable Open Educational Resources (OERs).

These principles define the instructional architecture of the model and remain applicable in face-to-face, hybrid, and fully online contexts.

---

# Replacing Physical Fieldwork

When direct urban exploration is not possible, authentic contexts can be recreated using digital resources.

Possible alternatives include:

- Google Street View;
- virtual museum visits;
- interactive city maps;
- open geospatial datasets;
- online photographic archives;
- local government open data portals;
- virtual cultural exhibitions;
- community-generated multimedia resources.

The emphasis should remain on observation, interpretation, and communication rather than passive content consumption.

---

# Adapting the Learning Tasks

Tasks should preserve authentic communicative purposes while taking advantage of digital environments.

| Learning objective | Online or hybrid adaptation |
|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Urban observation | Analyse Street View imagery, virtual tours, or digital maps. |
| Linguistic landscape | Document multilingual signage using openly available images. |
| Cultural interpretation | Compare digital representations of different communities or neighbourhoods. |
| Oral communication | Conduct interviews or discussions through videoconferencing platforms. |
| Collaborative production | Develop shared digital maps, multimedia guides, or interactive presentations. |

---

# Recommended Digital Tools

The instructional model can be implemented using widely available educational technologies.

| Purpose | Suggested tools |
|---------|-----------------|
| Collaboration | Microsoft Teams, Google Workspace, Moodle, Canvas |
| Mapping | Google Maps, ArcGIS StoryMaps |
| Multimedia production | Canva, Genially, Adobe Express, ThingLink |
| Communication | Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet |
| Reflection | Padlet, Wakelet, Microsoft OneNote, digital portfolios |

Technology should support learning objectives rather than determine instructional design.

---

# Assessment Considerations

Assessment principles remain unchanged.

Teachers should evaluate:

- communicative effectiveness;
- multimodal integration;
- collaboration;
- critical reflection;
- contextual interpretation;
- quality of learner-produced artefacts.

Digital participation should not replace evidence of learning but provide additional opportunities for documenting the learning process.

---

# Implementation Checklist

Before implementation, verify that:

- ☐ authentic communicative purposes remain central;
- ☐ learners actively investigate meaningful contexts;
- ☐ multimodal production is integrated throughout the project;
- ☐ collaboration is explicitly supported;
- ☐ assessment emphasises learning processes as well as products;
- ☐ digital tools are selected according to pedagogical needs rather than technological novelty.

---

# Key Takeaway

The instructional model is not dependent on physical fieldwork. Its educational value derives from authentic communication, situated inquiry, multimodal production, and collaborative learning. Online and hybrid environments can successfully support these principles when digital technologies are used to mediate—not replace—meaningful interaction with authentic contexts.
