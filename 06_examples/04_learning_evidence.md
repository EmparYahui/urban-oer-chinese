# Learning Evidence

## Purpose

This document explains how the learner-generated artefacts presented in this module constitute observable evidence of learning. Rather than being considered isolated classroom products, the artefacts document the development of linguistic, digital, multimodal, collaborative, and intercultural competences throughout the instructional sequence.

The examples should therefore be interpreted as evidence supporting both pedagogical practice and educational research.

---

# Evidence Across the Learning Process

The learner-generated artefacts document different stages of the instructional sequence rather than only final outcomes.

Examples include evidence of:

- initial language production;
- iterative drafting and revision;
- multimodal integration;
- collaborative knowledge construction;
- contextual interpretation;
- reflective learning;
- final public dissemination.

Together, these artefacts illustrate learning as a progressive and iterative process.

---

# Linguistic Evidence

The corpus documents observable aspects of second language development, including:

- vocabulary use in authentic contexts;
- contextualised written production;
- increasing control of communicative functions;
- revision following peer and teacher feedback;
- adaptation of language to communicative purposes.

The emphasis is placed on meaningful language use rather than isolated linguistic accuracy.

---

# Digital and Multimodal Evidence

The artefacts also demonstrate learners' ability to integrate multiple communicative modes.

Observable evidence includes:

- effective use of digital platforms;
- integration of written language with images and geolocation;
- production of multimedia resources;
- organisation of digital information;
- collaborative editing and publication.

These dimensions contribute to learners' digital and multimodal competence.

---

# Intercultural and Collaborative Evidence

Many artefacts document learners' interaction with authentic cultural environments and collaborative learning processes.

Observable evidence includes:

- interpretation of cultural practices;
- observation of multilingual environments;
- collaborative task completion;
- peer feedback;
- shared construction of learning resources.

These elements illustrate how language learning develops through social participation and authentic communication.

---

# Relationship with the Research Article

The artefacts presented in this corpus complement the analyses reported in the accompanying research article.

While the article synthesises the empirical findings, the examples presented here provide direct evidence of the learner productions that informed those analyses. Together, the article and the repository offer complementary perspectives on the same instructional intervention.

---

# Teaching Perspective

For teachers, the examples illustrate the types of evidence that can be collected throughout a task-based instructional sequence.

Rather than evaluating only final products, teachers are encouraged to document the complete learning process through successive learner productions, collaborative interactions, multimodal artefacts, and reflective activities.

---

# Key Takeaway

The educational value of the corpus lies not only in the quality of the final artefacts but also in its capacity to document learning as an observable, progressive, and evidence-based process. The examples therefore support both pedagogical decision-making and educational research.
