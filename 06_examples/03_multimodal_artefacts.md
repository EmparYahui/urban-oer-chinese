# Multimodal Artefacts

## Purpose

This document presents representative examples of the multimodal artefacts produced by learners throughout the instructional sequence. Rather than considering language as an exclusively verbal phenomenon, the instructional model encourages learners to integrate written texts, images, geolocation, audio, video, and digital media to communicate authentic experiences situated in real-world environments.

The examples illustrate how multimodal production becomes both a learning process and an observable learning outcome.

---

# What the Artefacts Show

The learner-generated artefacts combine multiple semiotic resources to construct meaning in authentic communicative situations.

Examples include:

- written production in the target language;
- geolocated photographs;
- digital maps;
- audio recordings;
- short videos;
- augmented reality content;
- multimodal annotations;
- collaborative digital documents.

Each artefact documents learners' ability to combine linguistic, visual, spatial, and digital resources in response to meaningful communicative tasks.

---

# Why Multimodality Matters

Within this instructional model, multimodality is not an additional technological component but a pedagogical principle.

Learners communicate by selecting and combining different modes according to communicative purpose, audience, and context. Language therefore becomes one element within a broader process of meaning-making that integrates visual, spatial, and digital resources.

This approach reflects authentic communication outside educational settings, where information is rarely conveyed through written language alone.

---

# Evidence of Learning

The artefacts provide observable evidence of learners' development in several complementary domains.

They document:

- language production in authentic contexts;
- multimodal communication;
- digital competence;
- spatial reasoning;
- intercultural observation;
- collaborative knowledge construction;
- reflective learning.

Taken together, the artefacts illustrate how multiple competences develop simultaneously through integrated communicative tasks.

---

# Teaching Perspective

For teachers, the examples demonstrate that multimodal production should not be understood as an additional classroom activity but as an integral component of task design.

The educational value lies in designing communicative situations in which learners naturally combine different modes to achieve authentic purposes.

Consequently, the specific technologies may change while the pedagogical principles remain stable.

---

# Student Perspective

For learners, the artefacts illustrate the diversity of possible responses to authentic communicative tasks.

They demonstrate that successful language learning extends beyond grammatical accuracy to include effective communication, contextual awareness, creativity, collaboration, and the appropriate integration of multiple communicative modes.

The examples are intended to inspire new productions rather than establish fixed models for replication.

---

# Possible Adaptations

Teachers may adapt multimodal production by using:

- different target languages;
- alternative digital platforms;
- local cultural environments;
- face-to-face, hybrid, or online instruction;
- individual or collaborative projects.

The pedagogical objective remains unchanged: learners construct meaning by integrating multiple communicative resources within authentic contexts.

# Key Takeaway

The multimodal artefacts presented in this corpus demonstrate that language learning can extend beyond written or spoken production to incorporate visual, spatial, digital, and collaborative modes of communication. Their educational value lies not in the technologies employed but in the pedagogical design that enables learners to construct meaning through authentic multimodal experiences.
