## Purpose

This example presents the collaborative digital map created by learners during the instructional sequence. The map illustrates how geolocated environments can become meaningful spaces for second language learning by integrating authentic observation, multimodal communication, and spatially situated language production.

Rather than functioning as a simple collection of locations, the map documents how learners connected language, place, and experience through a shared digital environment.

---

# What the Artefact Shows

The collaborative map combines multiple forms of learner-generated content linked to specific urban locations.

The examples include:

- geolocated photographs;
- short texts in the target language;
- multimodal annotations;
- location-based descriptions;
- evidence of collaborative editing;
- digital routes connecting different learning activities.

Together, these elements illustrate how learners documented authentic communicative experiences while interacting with the urban environment.

---

# Educational Value

The map demonstrates several characteristics of the instructional model.

### Situated Language Learning

Language is produced in direct relation to authentic places rather than through decontextualised classroom exercises.

### Multimodal Meaning-Making

Meaning emerges from the interaction of written language, photographs, spatial information, and digital media rather than from text alone.

### Collaborative Knowledge Construction

The final artefact results from multiple learner contributions that were progressively revised and integrated into a shared digital resource.

### Authentic Communication

Learners use the target language to document real experiences, describe places, and communicate information with identifiable purposes and audiences.

# Teaching Perspective

For teachers, this example illustrates how digital mapping can support task-based language learning without becoming the focus of instruction.

The pedagogical value lies not in the technology itself but in the way spatial environments stimulate observation, communication, collaboration, and multimodal production.

The same instructional approach can be adapted to different languages, educational levels, urban contexts, and digital platforms.

---

# Student Perspective

For learners, the map provides a concrete example of the type of multimodal artefact expected throughout the instructional sequence.

Rather than representing a model to be copied, it demonstrates how language, images, locations, and digital media can be combined to communicate authentic experiences and construct shared knowledge.

---

# Possible Adaptations

Teachers may adapt this activity by using:

- different target languages;
- alternative urban or community environments;
- virtual fieldwork through digital maps or Street View;
- institutional learning platforms;
- other geospatial technologies supporting collaborative mapping.

The instructional principles remain unchanged while the implementation can be adapted to local educational contexts.
