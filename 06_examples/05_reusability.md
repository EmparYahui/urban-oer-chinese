# Reusability

## Purpose

This document explains how learner-generated artefacts can extend their educational value beyond the original instructional sequence through responsible reuse, adaptation, and dissemination as Open Educational Resources (OERs).

Rather than functioning solely as assessment products, the artefacts become reusable learning resources capable of supporting future teaching, learning, teacher education, and educational research.

---

# From Learning Outcomes to Educational Resources

Within this instructional model, learner-generated artefacts fulfil multiple educational functions.

They simultaneously serve as:

- evidence of learning;
- assessment artefacts;
- examples of multimodal production;
- teaching resources;
- research data;
- reusable Open Educational Resources.

Publishing selected artefacts transforms individual learning products into shared educational knowledge.

---

# Principles of Reusability

Not every learner-generated artefact should be reused. Materials selected for publication should satisfy several pedagogical principles.

Reusable artefacts should be:

- educationally meaningful;
- technically accessible;
- clearly documented;
- ethically shared;
- appropriately licensed;
- adaptable to different educational contexts.

The educational value of an artefact depends not only on its quality but also on the clarity with which future users can interpret and adapt it.

---

# Ethical Considerations

Responsible reuse requires careful attention to ethical issues.

Before publication, artefacts should be:

- anonymised;
- reviewed for copyright compliance;
- checked for personal information;
- published with appropriate permissions;
- accompanied by contextual documentation.

These measures ensure that openness is compatible with responsible educational practice.

---

# Supporting Pedagogical Transfer

Reusable learner artefacts facilitate pedagogical transfer by helping educators understand how the instructional model operates in practice.

Rather than prescribing fixed classroom activities, the examples encourage teachers to:

- adapt learning tasks;
- redesign instructional sequences;
- modify assessment strategies;
- reinterpret multimodal production;
- apply the model to new educational contexts.

Reuse therefore promotes informed adaptation rather than procedural replication.

---

# Relationship with Open Educational Practices

The publication of learner-generated artefacts represents an example of Open Educational Practice (OEP).

Learning extends beyond the classroom through:

- authentic audiences;
- collaborative knowledge sharing;
- continuous improvement of educational resources;
- adaptation by other educators;
- contribution to wider educational communities.

Openness becomes part of the learning process rather than merely a method of dissemination.

---

# Teaching Perspective

For teachers, reusable artefacts reduce the effort required to implement innovative instructional designs.

Instead of starting from scratch, educators can analyse authentic learner productions, understand expected outcomes, and adapt activities to their own teaching contexts while preserving the pedagogical principles of the model.

---

# Key Takeaway

Learner-generated artefacts achieve their greatest educational value when they become reusable resources rather than remaining isolated classroom products. Their publication as Open Educational Resources supports continuous pedagogical improvement, encourages adaptation across educational contexts, and contributes to a broader culture of open and evidence-informed language education.
